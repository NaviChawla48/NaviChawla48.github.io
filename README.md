## Project Overview

Mental health has become one of the most pressing public health issues of the 21st century, and social media usage has grown explosively over the same period. But how connected are these two trends?

This tutorial investigates whether social media habits — including daily usage time, platform preferences, and self-reported effects on mood and focus — can predict whether someone seeks professional mental health treatment.

---

## Research Question

> **Can social media habits predict whether someone seeks professional mental health treatment?**

---

## Datasets

**1. OSMI Mental Health in Tech Survey (2014)**
- ~1,260 tech industry respondents
- Target variable: `treatment` (Yes/No)
- [Download from Kaggle](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/osmi/mental-health-in-tech-survey)

**2. Social Media & Mental Health Survey (2022)**
- 481 respondents with Likert-scale scores for depression, distraction, sleep issues, and self-comparison
- [Download from Kaggle](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/souvikahmed071/social-media-and-mental-health)

**3. WHO Mental Health Atlas (2020)**
- Country-level psychiatrist density, MH budget %, and legislation status
- Sourced from [WHO Atlas 2020 Table A3](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240036703) and [Our World in Data](https://ourworldindata.org/mental-health)
- Hardcoded directly in the notebook — no file download needed

---

## What This Tutorial Covers

- **ETL** — Loading, cleaning, and merging all three datasets with documented reasoning for every decision
- **EDA** — 7 visualizations revealing patterns across demographics, workplace context, social media behavior, and country-level infrastructure
- **Modeling** — Logistic Regression (L2-regularized) and Chi-Square Test of Independence, both motivated by EDA findings
- **Conclusions** — Key findings, limitations, and recommendations for future work

---

## Key Findings

- Workplace interference is the strongest predictor of treatment-seeking — respondents who report frequent work interference seek treatment at double the rate of those who report none
- Social media behavioral quality matters more than quantity — depression and distraction scores predict treatment-seeking; raw usage hours do not
- Younger adults report higher social-media-linked depression yet seek treatment less, suggesting barriers like stigma or cost
- Country-level psychiatric access has a modest positive effect but individual-level factors dominate
